Syllabus Outline

Core Chemistry Topics Covered

Our program is meticulously divided into progressive learning models designed to lay robust foundations before moving into advanced kinetics.

01

Introduction to Chemistry & Matter

Define chemistry, identify safety measures in laboratory settings, differentiate physical and chemical changes, and master heating processes using Bunsen burners.

02

Simple Classification of Substances

Examine separation methods including filtration, evaporation, crystallization, simple/fractional distillation, paper chromatography, and physical sorting mechanics.

03

Atomic Structure & The Periodic Table

Understand subatomic particles (protons, neutrons, electrons), calculate relative atomic mass, study isotopes, map electron configurations, and understand group trends.

04

Chemical Bonding & Structure

Differentiate covalent, ionic, giant metallic, and molecular lattice structures. Correlate molecular bonding properties to physical melting and boiling thresholds.

05

Acids, Bases, and Salts

Explore pH indicators, neutralization reactions, solubility rules of salts, step-by-step salt preparation procedures, and qualitative tests for cations and anions.

06

Gas Laws & Stoichiometry

Master Boyle's Law, Charles's Law, ideal gas equations, molecular volume configurations, the mole concept, balancing equations, and volumetric calculation setups.

Learning Goals

Chemistry Learning Objectives

At the conclusion of the Bwenke Chemistry program, each student will confidently demonstrate capabilities across these critical benchmarks:

  • Set up, light, and safely operate a Bunsen burner with luminous/non-luminous flames.
  • Differentiate elements, compounds, and mixtures, outlining isolation pathways.
  • Map complete electron configurations and predict bonding orientations of atoms.
  • Formulate balanced chemical and net ionic equations for acids, bases, and salts.
  • Perform precise stoichiometry volumetric molar calculations with zero formula guide assistance.
  • Handle volatile and acidic salts securely in analytical testing lab setups.
Laboratory Experiments

Required Practical Activities

Science is observed, not just imagined. Every student undertakes these core physical experimental modules within our safety-accredited science lab.

Experiment A

Acid-Base Titration

Determine the precise concentration of a sodium hydroxide solution using standardized hydrochloric acid and methyl orange or phenolphthalein pH indicators. Master titration calculations and meniscus leveling readings.

Experiment B

Laboratory Gas Preparation

Assemble apparatus setups to safely produce, dry, and collect key laboratory gases, including Oxygen (heating Potassium Chlorate/Manganese Dioxide catalyst), Hydrogen, and Carbon Dioxide. Conduct qualitative identification tests.

Experiment C

Qualitative Analysis (Salt Identification)

Investigate unknown inorganic white and colored powders. Perform flame tests, sodium hydroxide / aqueous ammonia reaction drops, and barium chloride tests to identify specific metal cations and halide/sulfate anions.

Collapsible Chemistry Lecture Notes

Subatomic blueprints: Every atom consists of a dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by shell configurations holding orbiting negative electrons. Mass is concentrated in the nucleus containing protons (relative mass 1, charge +1) and neutrons (relative mass 1, charge 0).

Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons inside an atom's nucleus. Determines elemental identity.

Mass Number (A): Total protons + neutrons inside nucleus. Isotopes are elements with identical Z but different A.

What is a Mole? One mole of any substance represents Avogadro's constant ($6.022 \times 10^{23}$) of representative particles (atoms, molecules, ions). It represents the relative atomic or formula mass of a substance expressed in grams.

Core Volumetric Formulas:

$$\text{Moles} = \frac{\text{Mass in grams}}{\text{Molar Mass (g/mol)}}$$

$$\text{Moles of gas at s.t.p} = \frac{\text{Volume in } dm^3}{22.4 dm^3}$$

Classification of Salts: Salts are formed when hydrogen ions in acids are partially or fully replaced by metal cations or ammonium ions. Acid salts retain replaceable hydrogen (e.g., $NaHCO_3$), while normal salts have no replaceable hydrogen ($Na_2CO_3$).

Solubility Rules Summary: All Nitrates, Sodium, Potassium, and Ammonium salts are highly soluble. All Chlorides are soluble except Silver and Lead Chloride. All Sulfates are soluble except Barium, Calcium, and Lead Sulfate.
